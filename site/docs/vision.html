Problem Statement #

Organizations are deploying AI agents at a pace that has outrun every governance practice they have. An AI agent is not a chatbot you can watch; it is an autonomous process that calls external APIs, reads and writes to databases, sends emails, executes code, and makes decisions — often with no human in the loop and no audit trail.

The result is a governance blindspot that is rapidly becoming a legal and financial liability.

The Legal Exposure Is Real and Growing

AI-related litigation is no longer theoretical. AI lawsuits grew 978% between 2021 and 2025, covering copyright infringement, discriminatory outputs, privacy violations, and autonomous agent decisions that harmed third parties. Courts are still setting precedent, but the direction is clear: companies will be held responsible for what their agents do.

In parallel, the insurance industry has moved to protect itself. ISO endorsement CG 40 47 — which explicitly excludes AI-generated harms from Commercial General Liability coverage — is now embedded in policies covering an estimated 82% of US property and casualty policies. Organizations that assumed their existing insurance covered AI incidents are discovering it does not.

Insurance Gap

The CGL exclusion language in CG 40 47 is explicit: coverage is denied for bodily injury, property damage, or personal injury "arising out of, related to, or in any way involving" the use of AI systems. Dedicated AI coverage from specialty insurers exists, but underwriters have no standardized data on which to base risk assessments. The result is either no coverage, or premiums priced for the worst case.

Regulatory Pressure Is Not Theoretical — It Has Deadlines

The EU AI Act's high-risk AI obligations take effect in August 2026. Organizations deploying AI in hiring, credit scoring, education, critical infrastructure, or law enforcement face mandatory conformity assessments, technical documentation, human oversight requirements, and incident reporting obligations — with fines up to €30M or 6% of global annual revenue.

In the United States, 38 states adopted approximately 100 AI-related legislative measures in 2025 alone. While no single federal framework exists, the patchwork of state laws creates compliance complexity that grows with every new deployment.

Compliance Clock

SOC 2 auditors are already asking the question: "How do you govern your AI agents?" As of 2026, there is no standardized answer and no off-the-shelf tool to produce one. Every engagement becomes a custom engagement — at consulting rates, not SaaS rates.

There Is No Self-Serve Tool

The most important feature of the current market is what does not exist. Every governance and compliance vendor in this space — without exception — is "contact sales" at enterprise price points starting at $100,000 per year. There is no tool an engineering team can install in 30 seconds, run against their codebase, and receive an actionable risk report from. That gap is the opportunity.

02

The Solution #

Agent Shield is a two-layer product: a free, open-source CLI that creates distribution and trust, paired with a paid platform for the teams that need continuous monitoring, team collaboration, and compliance reporting.

Layer 1 — The CLI (Free, Open Source)

Written in Rust for performance and binary distribution, the Agent Shield CLI does one thing extremely well: it scans a codebase and tells you what your AI agents can do, what risk they carry, and what compliance gaps exist. No configuration required. No account. No data leaves the machine.

  • Agent discovery across 10 frameworks: LangChain, LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen, OpenAI Assistants, Anthropic MCP, Anthropic Agent SDK, AWS Bedrock Agents, Vercel AI SDK, and custom agent patterns
  • Risk scoring 0–100 based on autonomy level, tool access, data permissions, guardrail coverage, and blast radius
  • Compliance mapping to NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act, and OWASP Agentic Top 10 — with specific control references, not vague guidance
  • SARIF output for CI/CD integration — block deployments of agents that exceed defined risk thresholds
  • Runs in under 30 seconds on a full enterprise codebase
Design Philosophy

The CLI is explicitly designed to feel like npm audit. Every developer already knows that workflow. A governance tool that developers actually run is infinitely more valuable than an enterprise platform they avoid. Distribution through developer trust, not enterprise sales.

Layer 2 — The Platform (Paid, SaaS)

For teams that need more than a point-in-time scan, the Agent Shield platform provides continuous monitoring, team collaboration, and the audit-ready documentation that compliance frameworks demand.

  • Web dashboard with per-agent risk history, finding trends, and remediation tracking
  • Continuous monitoring — re-scans triggered by code changes, with drift alerts when agent risk profiles change
  • PDF compliance reports formatted for auditors and board-level risk reviews
  • Team collaboration — assign findings, track remediation, maintain evidence chains for SOC 2 and ISO 42001
  • Compliance templates that grow with each new regulation added to the library

Why Now #

Timing is the most important variable in market entry. Five conditions are converging in 2026 that make this moment uniquely suited for Agent Shield's approach — and that make waiting genuinely costly.

1. The EU AI Act Deadline Is a Hard Forcing Function

August 2026 is not a soft target. It is a statutory date with fines up to €30M or 6% of global annual revenue for non-compliant high-risk AI deployments. Organizations deploying AI in finance, healthcare, HR, or critical infrastructure are actively seeking documentation they can show regulators. The window between "preparing now" and "scrambling at the last minute" closes in approximately six months.

2. State-Level Regulation Creates Compliance Complexity at Scale

Thirty-eight US states adopted approximately 100 AI-related legislative measures in 2025. Unlike GDPR — which, despite its flaws, provided a single compliance target — US AI regulation is fragmenting. An organization operating in multiple states faces an overlapping patchwork of obligations. Tools that map agent risk to multiple regulatory frameworks simultaneously become dramatically more valuable as the number of frameworks grows.

3. Insurance Exclusions Are Forcing Documentation

When ISO CGL exclusion CG 40 47 removes AI from standard coverage, the immediate buyer response is to seek specialty AI coverage. Specialty insurers are asking the same question that SOC 2 auditors are asking: what controls do you have over your AI agents? An audit report from Agent Shield is an answer. Without that report, the answer is "none" — which means either no coverage or a premium priced for maximum exposure.

4. Agent Framework Standardization Has Made Scanning Feasible

Two years ago, the AI agent ecosystem was too fragmented to scan systematically. Today, Model Context Protocol (MCP) and Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocols are emerging as standards. The 10 major frameworks Agent Shield targets represent the overwhelming majority of production agent deployments. Scanning is now a tractable engineering problem, not an impossible one.

5. The Auditor Question Has No Answer Yet

SOC 2 auditors are currently asking "how do you govern your AI agents?" as an informational inquiry. By 2027, it will be a scored control. Every organization that has deployed AI agents without governance infrastructure will face a retroactive compliance remediation project. The cost of addressing this proactively — with a $1,500/month tool — is trivially small compared to the cost of addressing it reactively, with consultants, after an audit finding or an incident.

The Compounding Moat

The business case for Agent Shield is not just a product — it is a data asset that becomes more valuable over time. Each scan that runs through the platform contributes to a structured dataset of agent risk profiles, tool configurations, guardrail patterns, and remediation outcomes. That dataset has three layers of compounding value:

  • Year 1–2
    Benchmark data Risk scores are more meaningful when compared to a baseline. "Your CustomerSupportBot scores 72/100" is less actionable than "72/100, which is higher than 89% of similar agents in production." That benchmark only exists with network data.
  • Year 2–3
    Compliance template library Each new regulation added to the compliance library increases value for all existing customers. The EU AI Act templates, once built, are available to every paying customer at zero marginal cost. The library grows; the cost per customer does not.
  • Year 3+
    Actuarial dataset Structured, longitudinal risk data on AI agent deployments is precisely what the specialty insurance market needs to price policies accurately. A partnership with one or two AI insurance underwriters — providing anonymized risk data as an actuarial input — turns the compliance platform into the foundation of the AI insurance market's data infrastructure.

Distribution Moat

The open-source CLI is not a marketing decision — it is a distribution strategy. Every engineer who installs the CLI and runs a scan is a qualified lead. They have already experienced the product, already seen the output, and already understand the value. The conversion funnel from CLI user to paid platform customer is shorter and cheaper than any inbound or outbound sales motion.

The CLI also creates a category-definition moat. If "agent-shield scan" becomes the default way engineers think about AI agent risk — the way "npm audit" is the default way they think about dependency vulnerabilities — the switching cost becomes the cognitive cost of changing a familiar workflow. That is a durable moat.

The Long Game

The vision is not to be acquired by a platform vendor in three years, though that outcome is available. The vision is to be the infrastructure layer for AI governance — the tool that every compliance team uses, the data that every AI insurer needs, and the framework that every auditor references. That position, once established, is extraordinarily difficult to displace.
